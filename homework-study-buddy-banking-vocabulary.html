🏦 At the Bank

Mr. Harris went to his local branch to review his finances after retiring. The advisor began by checking his cash account, which he used for daily spending, and his savings account, where he kept money aside for emergencies.

The advisor explained that his monthly pension arrives as a disbursement from his old workplace fund, and reminded him that any interest on his savings is subject to tax. She also warned him not to deplete his savings account too quickly, since it was his main safety net.

Mr. Harris mentioned he sometimes wanted to try investing. The advisor suggested a brokerage account for buying shares, and explained that a margin account would let him borrow money to invest — but warned this was riskier. She showed him how to check the yield and overall ROI of different funds before choosing one.

Finally, she reminded him to top up his cash account balance before his bills went out, so he wouldn't accidentally max out his overdraft limit and pay an unwanted overdraft fee.

Q1. What two accounts did the advisor check first, and what was each one for?
✅ His cash account (daily spending) and his savings account (money set aside for emergencies).
Q2. Where does Mr. Harris's monthly income come from, and what should he remember about it?
✅ It comes from a disbursement from his old workplace pension fund, and the interest on his savings is subject to tax.
Q3. What is the difference between a brokerage account and a margin account, according to the advisor?
✅ A brokerage account is for buying and selling shares with your own money; a margin account lets you borrow money to invest, which is riskier.
Q4. Why did the advisor tell him to top up his cash account before his bills went out?
✅ So that he wouldn't max out his overdraft limit and end up paying an overdraft fee.
Q5. TRUE or FALSE?  "The advisor encouraged him to spend his savings quickly."
