Q1. Which sentence (A, B or C) shows certainty (the speaker is sure)?
Q2. Which sentence shows possibility (the speaker isn't sure)?
Q3. Which sentence shows impossibility (the speaker is sure it's NOT true)?

Grammar rule — Speculation & Deduction

Certainty (sure it's true): must + verb  or  must have + past participle.

Impossibility (sure it's NOT true): can't + verb  or  can't have + past participle.

Possibility (not sure): may / might / could + verb  or  may have / might have / could have + past participle.

  • Use the plain form (must be) for present situations.
  • Use have + past participle (must have been) for past deductions.
  • Can is NOT used for positive deduction — only must.

⚡ The Hustle Generation

Meet Dan. He is 34, a product manager, and his week is a blur of stand-ups, customer calls and late-night roadmaps. His routine feels almost humdrum — same desk, same coffee, same alerts pinging at times he should be resting. His younger colleague, Maja, is 24. She watched Dan for a week and decided he must be close to burning out.

"He can't have taken a proper break in months," she told HR. Dan, in turn, looked at Maja's eight-hour sleeps and her twice-weekly gym slot and decided she might be lagging behind. He assumed her extra rest could have cost her a promotion. But HR's review showed the opposite: Maja had shipped two features that quarter.

Maja gently suggested Dan may have confused long hours with real output. "You must have been exhausted when you sent that memo at 3 a.m.," she said. Dan admitted the burn midnight oil habit was no longer all the rage in the team. To strike a balance, he started logging off at six. After a week he must have felt better — at least, he finally stopped complaining that the alerts got on his nerves.

Now Maja is the one bringing home the bacon on the new project, and Dan takes a real lunchbreak. He can't be in perpetual stress any more — or so his team hopes. Either way, the two generations might just have learned something from one another.

Q1. Why does Maja think Dan must be close to burning out?
✅ She observed his humdrum routine of long hours, alerts at all times, and late-night roadmaps.
Q2. Why can't the missing features be explained by Maja lagging behind?
✅ HR's review showed the opposite — Maja had shipped two features that quarter, despite her breaks.
Q3. Which underlined sentence expresses the strongest certainty about the past?
Q4. TRUE or FALSE?  "Dan thought Maja's extra rest had cost her a promotion."
Q5. What may Dan have confused with real output, and how did he reset?
✅ He may have confused long hours with real output. He reset by logging off at six and taking a real lunchbreak.

Certainty — must▾
Use must + verb for present certainty, must have + past participle for past certainty.
"She looks shattered — she must have burned the midnight oil."   "He must be the new PM."
Impossibility — can't▾
Use can't + verb for present impossibility, can't have + past participle for past.
"She is on leave — she can't be in the stand-up."   "He replied at 7 a.m. — he can't have slept in."
Possibility — may / might / could▾
Use may / might / could + verb for present possibility, may have / might have / could have for past.
"The team might have missed the deadline."   "He could be running late."
Common mistakes to avoid▾
✗ must has → ✓ must have (always "have", never "has" after a modal).
✗ can have spent (for certainty) → ✓ must have spent.
✗ might missed → ✓ might have missed.
✗ Using must for a guess → ✓ use might / could / may.
