Grammar rule — Speculation & Deduction

Certainty (sure it's true): must + verb  or  must have + past participle.

Impossibility (sure it's NOT true): can't + verb  or  can't have + past participle.

Possibility (not sure): may / might / could + verb  or  may have / might have / could have + past participle.

  • Use the plain form (must be) for present situations.
  • Use have + past participle (must have been) for past deductions.
  • Can is NOT used for positive deduction — only must.

🏦 The Mystery on the Statement

When Mrs. Calder opened her cash account app one morning, she found three transfers she did not recognise. The balance was far lower than it should have been. She must have checked the statement five times, but the transactions were still there. She immediately called her branch and asked to speak to an advisor.

The advisor looked at the records. "It can't have been a system error," she said carefully, "because the transfers were each confirmed by an SMS code. Someone might have used your phone, or you could have given the code to someone by mistake." She added that the money had gone to a brokerage account in another country.

Mrs. Calder was sure she had never opened such an account. "Then it must have been opened in your name by someone else," the advisor said. "We may have to freeze the savings account and check the recent disbursements." She warned that Mrs. Calder might be subject to an investigation, and that the missing funds can't have disappeared on their own.

The advisor ended the call with a question. "Who could have had access to your device?" Mrs. Calder paused. Her grandson had borrowed her phone last weekend. He must have seen her banking password. Or maybe not. It might just be a coincidence — but the bank would soon find out.

Certainty — must▾
Use must + verb for present certainty, must have + past participle for past certainty.
"Her savings account is empty — she must have spent it all."   "He must be the new account holder."
Impossibility — can't▾
Use can't + verb for present impossibility, can't have + past participle for past.
"She retired — she can't be receiving a salary."   "He was on holiday — he can't have made the transfer."
Possibility — may / might / could▾
Use may / might / could + verb for present possibility, may have / might have / could have for past.
"The bank might have delayed the disbursement."   "The yield could be lower next quarter."
Common mistakes to avoid▾
✗ must has → ✓ must have (always "have", never "has" after a modal).
✗ can have spent (for certainty) → ✓ must have spent.
✗ might processed → ✓ might have processed.
✗ Using must for a guess → ✓ use might / could / may.
